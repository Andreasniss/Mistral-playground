The history of artificial intelligence begins in antiquity, with myths, stories, and
rumors of artificial beings endowed with intelligence or consciousness by master
craftsmen. The seeds of modern AI were planted by philosophers who attempted to
describe the process of human thinking as the mechanical manipulation of symbols.
This work culminated in the invention of the programmable digital computer in the
1940s, a machine based on the abstract essence of mathematical reasoning. This device
and the ideas behind it inspired a handful of scientists to begin seriously discussing
the possibility of building an electronic brain.

The field of AI research was founded at a workshop held on the campus of Dartmouth
College during the summer of 1956. Those who attended would become the leaders of AI
research for decades. Many of them predicted that a machine as intelligent as a human
being would exist in no more than a generation, and they were given millions of dollars
to make this vision come true. Eventually it became obvious that commercial developers
and government researchers had grossly underestimated the difficulty of the project.
In 1974, in response to the criticism from James Lighthill and ongoing pressure from
Congress, the U.S. and British Governments stopped funding undirected research into
artificial intelligence, and the difficult years that followed would later be known as
an "AI winter."

In the early 1980s, AI research was revived by the commercial success of expert
systems, a form of AI program that simulated the knowledge and analytical skills of
human experts. By 1985, the market for AI had reached over a billion dollars. Along
with this came a second AI winter in the late 1980s. In the 1990s and 2000s, AI
achieved its greatest successes, albeit somewhat behind the scenes. Artificial
intelligence is used for logistics, data mining, medical diagnosis, and many other
areas throughout the technology industry.

Deep learning began to dominate benchmark results around 2012. AlexNet, a convolutional
neural network trained on ImageNet, dramatically outperformed all prior image
classification systems. In 2017, the transformer architecture was introduced, enabling
models to process sequences in parallel and attend to long-range dependencies with
unprecedented efficiency. This unlocked a new paradigm: large language models trained
on massive corpora of text could, with sufficient scale, exhibit emergent capabilities
such as few-shot reasoning, code generation, and multilingual translation — none of
which were explicitly programmed.

By the early 2020s, models like GPT-3, PaLM, and Mistral demonstrated that scaling
laws held across many orders of magnitude, and that a single general-purpose model
could outperform narrow task-specific systems on a wide variety of benchmarks. The
open-weight movement, championed in part by Mistral AI, challenged the assumption
that frontier capability required closed, proprietary systems, releasing competitive
models that researchers and developers could inspect, fine-tune, and deploy without
restriction. This shift accelerated the pace of applied AI research globally and
brought the question of AI governance, safety, and societal impact to the forefront
of both academic and public discourse.
